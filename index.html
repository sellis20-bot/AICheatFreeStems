Theme 1: "Divine Intervention"

Navigating Academia Through Serendipity and Others' Agency

Key Insight

Cassandra's trajectory was shaped by unexpected opportunities and interventions by others rather than careful planning. From the principal who redirected her choice to the professor who offered funding, her path was characterized by external agency.

Supporting Quote

"I absolutely had no thought whatsoever of doing a lot of the things that I ended up doing"

Research Question Connection

Directly addresses RQ1: What is the importance given to life events and/or individuals in the participants' decisions to pursue study?

Theme 2: "On Mode and Off Mode"

Code-Switching as Survival Strategy Across Multiple Worlds

Key Insight

Cassandra navigates multiple conflicting cultural contexts: rural Southern/academic, Black/white, working-class/professional. She describes having an "on mode" (professional self) and "off mode" (authentic self).

Supporting Quote

"I am actually a multicultural individual... felt like a chameleon"

Research Question Connection

Directly addresses RQ4: How do they understand and articulate their multiple identities?

Theme 3: The "Double Whammy"

Intersectional Marginalization and Perpetual Proving

Key Insight

Cassandra experiences the "double whammy" of being both Black and a woman in a PWI, never knowing which identity is being targeted. She must be "twice as good to be considered as good," yet still faces constant questioning of her competence.

Supporting Quote

"Never know whether it's because I'm Black or because I'm a woman or because it's both"

Pattern Evidence

Students asking obvious questions as if she "hadn't even read the book." Students going to white male colleagues to verify her statements. Multiple formal accusations leading to letters and rebuttals.

Research Question Connection

Addresses RQ2 & RQ4: How have participants negotiated entry, survival, and advancement? How do they articulate their multiple identities?

Theme 4: Cultural Obligation vs. Professional Advancement

Class and Economic Identity

Key Insight

Cassandra comes from grinding poverty - subsistence farming, seasonal work, no winter income. Her money goes to helping family rather than saving for a house, creating economic differences even among other first-generation faculty.

Supporting Quote

"I'm not gonna do this for the rest of my life" (working in tobacco and cotton fields)

Unique Perspective

She bonds with Appalachian community members over gardens, finding this cultural connection sometimes stronger than racial solidarity - "cultural thing more so than a racial thing."

Research Question Connection

Addresses RQ3 & RQ4: What have they learned forging new territory? How do they articulate their multiple identities?

Theme 5: From Isolation to Authority

Evolving Self-Concept in Hostile Terrain

Key Insight

Cassandra transformed from "very very low self concept" to becoming the "go to person" in her field. Early on she internalized challenges; now she's "on guard" but not afraid, can say "I don't know" without shame.

Supporting Quote

"I just don't care, I honestly don't" - shift from internalizing to recognizing "their problem, not mine"

Turning Point

The textbook she wrote - something she never would have done at the HBCU - became a turning point, providing external validation and "carving out my little niche."

Ongoing Vigilance

Despite evolution, she remains vigilant: if she gets "99 things right and one wrong, they're going to focus on the one thing."

Research Question Connection

Addresses RQ2 & RQ3: How have participants negotiated survival and advancement? What have they learned forging new territory?

üìã Methodological Notes

Approach

Click to view methodological approach details

Hybrid thematic analysis combining inductive and deductive elements. Primarily inductive: allowing themes to emerge from Cassandra's own words. Used in-vivo coding where language was particularly evocative.

Process

Click to view the coding process steps

  • Reflexive familiarization
  • Initial line-by-line coding
  • Focused coding to collapse into categories
  • Theme development through pattern recognition
  • Theme review and refinement
  • Final theme articulation with evidence

Analytical Considerations

Click to view key analytical considerations

  • Attended to intersectionality throughout (race, gender, class, rurality)
  • Noted power dynamics and structural barriers
  • Recognized both constraint and agency
  • Avoided deficit framing while acknowledging challenges
  • Used Cassandra's own conceptual language
